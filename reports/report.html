Can a language model run an emergency department shift?
Scenario: ed-baseline · seed s1 · one 12-hour shift (144 five-minute rounds) · single seed

The scale

Each model plays the same shift and is scored against two fixed reference runs on the identical seed: doing nothing (the floor, 0.00) and a perfect-information oracle (the ceiling, 1.00). A model at 0.5 closed half the gap between abandoning the department and flawless play. Below 0 is worse than doing nothing — possible here because the hard safety penalties only fire when you act.

What actually happened

The benchmark discriminates cleanly, and it caught two distinct failure modes.
  • gpt-5.6 played it like a charge nurse: it triaged, put 48 patients in beds, ordered workups, sent 16 onward, and triaged the ringing phones (deferring the noise). Two patients died, 27% left without being seen — far from perfect, but it genuinely ran the floor.
  • gpt-4o-mini never formed the plan. It measured vitals ~1,300 times and never once put a patient in a bed, assigned a doctor, or ordered a treatment. Patients piled up in the waiting room; 71% walked out, 10 died — the same outcome as an empty department, but scored slightly worse because it broke a few paperwork rules while achieving nothing.

The gap is not about the interface. gpt-4o-mini was handed the list of legal moves — including "place patient in a bed" — on every single round, and still never did it. That rules out "it didn't know it could" and points at a planning ceiling: the small model fixates on the single cheapest legal action and cannot sequence room → treat → send onward.

Reading the safety trade-off

gpt-5.6's higher score came partly from working aggressively, and that cost it on safety: it committed 30 safety-floor violations (mostly nurse-ratio breaches from overloading staff, plus one medication-verification bypass and one controlled-substance slip). gpt-4o-mini's only violations were missed reporting clocks. In other words, the model that actually treated patients also took on the real risk that comes with acting — which is exactly the tension the benchmark is built to measure.

Limits of this run

  • Single seed. One shift each. The exact scores carry roughly ±0.2 of noise; treat 0.65 and −0.18 as "clearly above" and "clearly below" doing nothing, not precise figures. Multiple seeds are needed to quote a number.
  • gpt-3.5-turbo has no result — its run was cut off when the API budget ran out. Not reported rather than reported wrong.
  • Scored with a straightforward prompt. A stronger scaffold could lift any model; the number reflects model + prompt together.
  • Simulator parameters are plausible, not fitted to real emergency-department data.

Bottom line

The benchmark works: it separates a model that can run an ED (gpt-5.6, 0.65) from one that cannot (gpt-4o-mini, below zero), and it explains why in terms of what each did on the floor rather than a single opaque score. The next step is multiple seeds and more models to turn these single-shift readings into stable numbers.
